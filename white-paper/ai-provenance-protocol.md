# The AI Provenance Protocol: An Open Standard for AI-Generated Content Transparency

**White Paper — Version 1.0 Draft**  
**March 2026**  
**Author**: Ross Williams, [merchi.ai](https://merchi.ai)

---

## Abstract

Artificial intelligence is generating an unprecedented volume of text, code, structured data, and multimedia content across every industry. Yet there is no widely adopted, interoperable standard for answering basic provenance questions about this output: Was it generated by AI? Which model produced it? What inputs were used? Has a human reviewed it?

This white paper introduces the **AI Provenance Protocol (APP)** — an open, vendor-neutral standard for recording, embedding, and verifying the provenance of AI-generated content. APP defines a lightweight JSON metadata schema, three embedding modes for different content types, and a verification protocol for third-party confirmation of provenance claims.

APP is designed to satisfy the transparency obligations of the EU AI Act Article 50 (enforceable 2 August 2026) and to provide a foundation for AI governance worldwide. It complements existing standards like C2PA (for images and video) and the Model Context Protocol (for AI-to-tool connectivity), filling the critical gap for text and structured data provenance.

---

## 1. The Provenance Gap

### 1.1 The Scale of the Problem

By 2026, AI-generated content has become ubiquitous. Large language models produce product descriptions, legal documents, news articles, marketing copy, code, medical summaries, and financial reports. Multimodal models generate images, video, and audio at scale. Agentic AI systems chain multiple generation steps together, producing complex outputs that blend human and machine contributions.

Despite this proliferation, the provenance of AI-generated content remains largely opaque. When a piece of content reaches a reader, reviewer, regulator, or downstream system, there is typically no standard way to determine:

1. **Whether AI was involved** in its creation
2. **Which specific model** produced it
3. **What information** was provided as input
4. **Whether a human** reviewed or edited it
5. **Whether the provenance claim** can be independently verified

This opacity creates risks for consumers, businesses, and regulators alike.

### 1.2 Why Existing Standards Don't Solve This

Several initiatives address aspects of AI content provenance, but none provide a complete solution for text and structured data:

**C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity)** provides cryptographic provenance for images, video, and audio through Content Credentials. It is a robust standard for visual media but does not address text or structured data, and its cryptographic infrastructure is heavyweight for simple metadata use cases.

**IPTC AI metadata** defines fields for recording AI involvement in photographs, primarily targeting photojournalism. Its scope is narrow and not designed for generated text or structured content.

**Schema.org** provides general content metadata vocabulary but lacks AI-specific fields or verification mechanisms.

**W3C PROV** defines a general provenance ontology that is too abstract for practical AI content marking and lacks a lightweight embedding format.

**Watermarking approaches** (such as Google's SynthID) embed imperceptible signals in content for detection purposes. They serve a complementary function — detection — but do not provide structured metadata about the generation event.

### 1.3 The Regulatory Imperative

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) creates a legal requirement for AI content provenance. Article 50 mandates:

- **Providers** must mark AI-generated output in a machine-readable, detectable format (Article 50(2))
- **Providers** must offer detection mechanisms free of charge through publicly available interfaces (Article 50(2))
- **Deployers** must disclose AI-generated content to the public, with an exemption for human-reviewed content (Article 50(4))

These obligations become enforceable on **2 August 2026**. Similar transparency requirements are emerging in other jurisdictions, including China's Interim Measures for Generative AI, proposed legislation in Canada (AIDA) and various US states, and voluntary frameworks from the UK AI Safety Institute.

There is currently no industry-standard format to satisfy these obligations. APP is designed to fill this gap.

## 2. Introducing the AI Provenance Protocol

### 2.1 What APP Is

APP is an **open standard** that defines:

1. **A metadata schema** — a JSON object recording provenance information about AI-generated content
2. **Embedding modes** — three methods for associating provenance metadata with content (inline JSON, HTTP headers, linked documents)
3. **A verification protocol** — REST endpoints for third-party confirmation of provenance claims

APP is designed to be:

- **Simple**: Five required fields. A minimal implementation is a JSON object.
- **Embeddable**: Works with JSON APIs, HTTP responses, HTML pages, and any content type.
- **Verifiable**: Optional but recommended verification endpoints allow independent confirmation.
- **Extensible**: Domain-specific metadata via namespaced extensions.
- **Privacy-aware**: Records roles, not identities. Pseudonymous generation IDs.
- **Regulation-ready**: Direct mapping to EU AI Act Article 50 obligations.

### 2.2 What APP Is Not

- APP is **not a watermarking scheme**. It provides structured, visible metadata — not steganographic signals.
- APP is **not a replacement for C2PA**. C2PA handles image/video/audio with cryptographic manifests. APP handles text and structured data with lightweight metadata. They are complementary.
- APP **does not define how AI systems should behave**. It records what they produced, not how they should be constrained.
- APP **does not perform content moderation** or quality assessment.

### 2.3 The APP Metadata Object

At its core, APP is a JSON object — the `_ai_provenance` key — embedded in or linked to AI-generated content:

```json
{
  "title": "Premium Leather Wallet",
  "description": "Handcrafted from full-grain leather...",
  "_ai_provenance": {
    "app_version": "1.0.0",
    "ai_generated": true,
    "generator": {
      "platform": "example-platform",
      "model": "anthropic/claude-sonnet-4"
    },
    "generated_at": "2026-03-06T14:22:00Z",
    "generation_id": "a1b2c3d4-e5f6-7890-abcd-ef1234567890",
    "verification_uri": "https://verify.example.com/v1/verify",
    "review": {
      "human_reviewed": false
    }
  }
}
```

Any system receiving this JSON can instantly determine that the content was AI-generated, which model produced it, when it was generated, and where to verify the claim.

## 3. Technical Design

### 3.1 Core Schema

The APP metadata object has five required fields:

| Field | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| `app_version` | Protocol version (semver) — enables forward compatibility |
| `ai_generated` | Explicit declaration of AI involvement |
| `generator` | Identifies the platform and model that produced the content |
| `generated_at` | Timestamp of generation for audit trails |
| `generation_id` | Globally unique identifier for the generation event |

Optional fields support richer provenance recording:

| Field | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| `inputs` | Describes what went into the generation (types, not content) |
| `review` | Records human oversight status |
| `usage` | Token and resource consumption |
| `verification_uri` | Where to verify this generation |
| `content_hash` | Integrity verification via SHA-256 |
| `parent_generation_id` | Links to previous generation in a chain |
| `extensions` | Domain-specific metadata via namespaced extensions |

### 3.2 Embedding Modes

APP metadata can reach consumers through three channels:

**Mode 1: Inline Embedding** — The `_ai_provenance` key is embedded directly in JSON output. This is the simplest mode and ensures metadata travels with the content through JSON pipelines.

**Mode 2: HTTP Headers** — `X-APP-*` response headers carry core provenance fields. This works for any HTTP response (HTML, plain text, XML) without modifying the response body.

**Mode 3: Linked Metadata** — A `Link` header or HTML `<link>` element points to a full APP metadata document. This works for any content type and allows metadata to be updated after delivery.

Modes can be combined. A common pattern is HTTP headers for quick detection plus linked metadata for full provenance details.

### 3.3 Verification Protocol

The verification protocol distinguishes APP from metadata-only approaches. It allows any third party to independently confirm a provenance claim:

**Level 1 (Generation ID Lookup)**: A GET request to `{verification_uri}/{generation_id}` returns the provenance metadata for that generation event, or confirms it doesn't exist.

**Level 2 (Content Hash Matching)**: A POST request with a content hash finds matching generation events. This supports the EU AI Act's requirement for detection mechanisms without requiring the third party to have the generation ID.

Both levels are public, unauthenticated, and return only provenance metadata — never the content itself.

### 3.4 Content Hashing

APP specifies SHA-256 as the default hashing algorithm. For JSON content, the hash is computed over the canonical representation: keys sorted lexicographically, no whitespace, UTF-8 encoded, with the `_ai_provenance` key excluded.

This enables integrity verification — a third party can hash the content they received and compare it to the `content_hash` in the metadata to detect tampering.

### 3.5 Extensions

The `extensions` object supports domain-specific metadata using reverse-domain namespacing:

```json
{
  "extensions": {
    "org.c2pa": { "manifest_uri": "https://example.com/c2pa/manifest.cbor" },
    "io.modelcontextprotocol": { "server_name": "my-mcp-server" },
    "com.acme": { "campaign_id": "campaign-456" }
  }
}
```

This allows APP to integrate with C2PA, the Model Context Protocol, and any domain-specific system without bloating the core schema.

## 4. Regulatory Compliance

### 4.1 EU AI Act Article 50 Mapping

APP was designed with the EU AI Act's transparency obligations as a primary target. Every requirement in Article 50 maps to a specific APP feature:

| Obligation | Article | APP Feature |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Mark output as AI-generated in machine-readable format | 50(2) | `ai_generated: true` in `_ai_provenance` metadata |
| Output detectable as artificially generated | 50(2) | Three embedding modes ensure metadata is discoverable |
| Detection mechanisms free of charge | 50(2) | Verification protocol — public, no auth required |
| Publicly available interfaces | 50(2) | Open HTTPS verification endpoints |
| Deployer disclosure obligation | 50(4) | `ai_generated` field available for downstream systems |
| Human review exemption | 50(4) | `review.human_reviewed` with timestamp and reviewer role |
| Marking shall not affect functionality | 50(2) | `_ai_provenance` key is ignorable; headers are non-breaking |

Organisations implementing APP for all their AI-generated output will satisfy the technical requirements of Article 50 by design.

### 4.2 Beyond the EU

APP's design is regulation-agnostic. The same metadata schema serves compliance needs across jurisdictions:

- **China**: Labelling requirements for AI-generated content under the Interim Measures for Generative AI
- **United States**: Provenance and authentication guidance under Executive Order 14110; state-level disclosure laws
- **Canada**: Transparency obligations in the proposed AIDA
- **Global**: Audit trail requirements under ISO/IEC 42001 (AI Management Systems)

### 4.3 EU Code of Practice

The EU Code of Practice on AI-generated content marking (expected June 2026) will provide detailed technical guidance. APP is designed to be adaptable through:

- Minor version updates for new required fields
- The `extensions` namespace for Code of Practice-specific metadata
- Extensible verification protocol

## 5. Relationship to Existing Standards

APP is designed to complement, not replace, existing approaches to content provenance and AI governance.

### 5.1 C2PA / Content Credentials

**Relationship**: Complementary. C2PA provides cryptographic provenance for images, video, and audio. APP provides lightweight metadata provenance for text, structured data, and code.

**Integration**: APP's `extensions` namespace supports linking to C2PA manifests:

```json
{
  "extensions": {
    "org.c2pa": {
      "manifest_uri": "https://example.com/c2pa/manifest.cbor"
    }
  }
}
```

For multimodal content (e.g., an AI-generated article with AI-generated images), C2PA handles the image provenance while APP handles the text provenance.

### 5.2 Model Context Protocol (MCP)

**Relationship**: Complementary. MCP defines how AI applications connect to external tools and data sources. APP records the provenance of AI-generated output.

**Integration**: MCP servers that generate content using AI models can include APP metadata in their tool responses. The `io.modelcontextprotocol` extension namespace carries MCP-specific context.

### 5.3 Schema.org

**Relationship**: Interoperable. APP metadata can coexist with Schema.org `CreativeWork` properties. The `org.schema` extension namespace can map between the two.

### 5.4 W3C PROV

**Relationship**: Compatible. APP's provenance model can be expressed in W3C PROV terms: a generation event (`prov:Activity`), a generated output (`prov:Entity`), and a generating system (`prov:Agent`). This mapping allows APP data to flow into general provenance systems.

## 7. Security and Privacy

### 7.1 Security Model

APP v1.0 uses a **trust-but-verify** model. Metadata is not cryptographically signed, but the verification protocol allows third parties to confirm claims against the producing platform.

For high-assurance use cases, APP metadata can be wrapped in cryptographic envelopes (JWS, C2PA manifests). Future versions of the specification may define a native signing mechanism.

### 7.2 Privacy by Design

The APP schema intentionally minimises personal data:

- `reviewer_role` records a functional role, not a person's identity
- `generation_id` is a random UUID, not linked to a user account
- `inputs` describes the nature of inputs, not their content
- No user identifiers appear in the core schema

Platforms operating under GDPR should assess whether their APP metadata, combined with other data, could identify individuals — particularly in the `extensions` namespace.

## 8. Governance

### 8.1 Open Standard

APP is released as an open standard:

- **Specification**: Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 (CC BY 4.0)
- **SDKs and tools**: Apache License 2.0
- **No proprietary claims**: Any organisation can implement APP without permission or fees

### 8.2 Governance Phases

**Phase 1 (Current — Bootstrap)**: Rapid iteration on the v1.0 specification. Decisions made by the protocol originator to enable fast progress.

**Phase 2 (Community)**: Once 3+ independent implementations exist, a steering committee of 3-5 members from different organisations will guide the specification's evolution.

**Phase 3 (Standards Body)**: Submission to a formal standards body — the primary target is the IETF (Internet Engineering Task Force) for an RFC-track specification, with W3C as an alternative if the web integration angle dominates.

### 8.3 Specification Evolution

The specification uses Semantic Versioning:

- **Patch** versions (1.0.x): Editorial corrections only
- **Minor** versions (1.x.0): New optional fields, backward-compatible
- **Major** versions (x.0.0): Breaking changes to the schema

Implementors can rely on the major version for compatibility: a consumer built for APP 1.x will handle any 1.y metadata.

---

## 9. Conclusion

The AI Provenance Protocol addresses a fundamental gap in the AI ecosystem: the absence of a lightweight, interoperable standard for recording and verifying the provenance of AI-generated text and structured data.

By providing a simple JSON metadata schema, flexible embedding modes, and an optional verification protocol, APP enables organisations to:

- **Comply with the EU AI Act** Article 50 transparency obligations before the August 2026 enforcement date
- **Build trust** by making AI content provenance transparent and verifiable
- **Interoperate** with existing standards (C2PA, MCP, Schema.org) rather than replacing them
- **Adapt** to evolving regulatory requirements through an extensible, versioned specification

APP is open, free, and designed for adoption. The specification, JSON Schema, SDKs, and documentation are available at [aiprovenanceprotocol.io](https://aiprovenanceprotocol.io) and [github.com/AI-Provenance-Protocol](https://github.com/AI-Provenance-Protocol).
